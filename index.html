01 Building a "True" Enterprise Risk Inventory at Scale BMO U.S. ▾

Context

As head of the U.S. Risk Identification function at BMO, I was responsible for strengthening the bank's ability to consistently identify, assess, and prioritize enterprise risks—and translate that view into governance-ready decision support.

The Challenge

The organization needed an enterprise risk inventory that was more than a list. The goal was a repeatable, defensible process producing: standardized risk statements; a consistent approach for quantifying impact and determining materiality; clear first- and second-line ownership; a defined review and challenge cadence; and training to drive consistent participation across stakeholders.

What I Did

  • Inventory design and standardization. Established a standardized risk statement structure so risks were comparable and clearly articulated. Implemented consistent assessment fields and methodology for impact evaluation and materiality determination.
  • Ownership and accountability. Identified and documented owners in both the first line (business) and second line (risk), clarifying accountability and escalation paths.
  • Governance: review and challenge. Designed a structured challenge process that enabled consistent scrutiny and improved assessment quality across risk owners and SMEs.
  • Training and enablement. Developed and rolled out training to improve adoption, increase consistency, and reduce variability in how risks were interpreted and scored.

Scale

1,400+ risks facilitated in partnership with 50+ executives and SMEs.

Outcomes

Year one created a true risk inventory for BMO U.S. for the first time—with clearer risk statements, quantified impact methodology, and a defensible approach to materiality and ownership. Year two matured the program through expanded coverage, increased standardization, greater quantitative rigor, and deeper use of existing data sources.

Why This Matters

A credible risk inventory is the backbone of ERM. This work improved the bank's ability to prioritize risks consistently, align risk discussions across leaders, establish a clear accountability model, and support downstream processes like risk appetite, scenario design, and Board reporting.

02 Redesigning Risk Taxonomy + Multi-Level Roll-Up Architecture BMO ▾

Context

A risk inventory only becomes decision-useful when risks can be classified consistently and rolled up across business structures. At BMO, I led work to transform both the risk taxonomy and the organizational assessment structure used for risk aggregation.

The Challenge

Two foundational ERM problems had to be solved simultaneously: (1) taxonomy clarity—categories needed higher granularity and stronger definitions to reduce overlap; and (2) aggregation across organizational complexity—assessments needed to roll up reliably from assessment unit → line of business → operating group → entity → enterprise, ultimately spanning 12 entities, subsidiaries, and affiliates.

What I Did

  • Taxonomy redesign. Increased granularity, strengthened mutual exclusivity and collective exhaustiveness, improved definitions, and drove consensus so stakeholders shared a clear understanding of each risk category.
  • Risk ownership expansion. As taxonomy became more granular, identified additional first- and second-line owners aligned to the refined categories.
  • Organizational assessment hierarchy. Created an organizational taxonomy enabling risks assessed at the unit level to roll up systematically through the full enterprise hierarchy—designed to scale from the initial BMO U.S. / BMO Enterprise scope to a broader multi-entity footprint.

Outcomes

This work materially increased the usefulness of ERM outputs by improving classification consistency, comparability of assessments across units, transparency of ownership at the right organizational level, and reliability of roll-up reporting for senior leadership. It created a scalable ERM foundation supporting risk appetite mapping, reporting, and enterprise-level governance.

Why This Matters

Most ERM programs fail not because risk professionals can't identify risks, but because they can't classify them consistently or aggregate them across organizational complexity. This work addressed those root causes directly.

Why This Matters

For regulated financial institutions, stress testing and capital-related reporting are areas where small process gaps become major supervisory issues. This work demonstrated the ability to stabilize high-risk reporting processes, institutionalize control discipline, and deliver outcomes that reduce regulatory and audit risk.

01 Why Most Risk Heat Maps Fail (and What to Do Instead) ▾

Risk heat maps are everywhere in financial services. They look clean, they're easy to socialize, and they give leadership a sense that risk is being "managed." The problem is that heat maps often convert complex uncertainty into a tidy picture that feels quantitative—but isn't.

Douglas Hubbard's The Failure of Risk Management makes a blunt point: when we treat ordinal categories ("low/medium/high") like numbers, we get outputs that are mathematically meaningless and operationally misleading. In practice, that shows up as two risks with wildly different potential losses landing in the same red box, or a "high likelihood / low impact" risk outranking a lower likelihood event that could materially impair capital.

The hidden flaw: ordinal scales aren't arithmetic

A 1–5 scale is not automatically numeric in a useful way. If "impact = 5" is simply "catastrophic," you can't assume it's five times worse than "impact = 1." When we multiply "likelihood × impact," we're doing arithmetic on labels. That doesn't mean qualitative assessment is useless. It means we should stop confusing it with measurement.

A better approach: decision-grade quantification

You don't need perfect data to improve a decision. You need enough precision to choose between alternatives. A practical upgrade path:

  • Standardize the risk statement. Make risk statements specific enough to quantify. Bad: "Credit risk increases." Better: "Increased CRE refinancing failures drive losses above baseline."
  • Define an economic impact range. Even a wide range is better than none: "$5M–$25M expected loss under adverse conditions" or "0–50 bps capital ratio impact."
  • Use calibrated judgment. Most experts are overconfident. Calibration training—simple exercises that teach people what "90% confidence" actually means—produces immediate improvement in estimation quality.
  • Simulate rather than pretend. Monte Carlo simulation doesn't require fancy data science. It requires ranges, distributions (even rough), and iteration. The output is not a "perfect number," but an honest representation of uncertainty.

What this changes in an ERM program

When you move from heat maps to ranges and distributions, materiality decisions become defensible, prioritization becomes traceable, and "risk appetite" stops being abstract and becomes measurable thresholds. Most boards actually prefer: top risks ranked by estimated loss distribution, sensitivities ("what drives the tail?"), and early warning indicators tied to measurable thresholds.

Practical takeaway

Treat heat maps as communication tools, not analytic tools. Build your real prioritization engine on standardized risk statements, quantified impact ranges, calibrated estimates, and simulation outputs. That's how you get risk reporting that informs decisions instead of simply reassuring stakeholders.

02 "If You Can't Measure It, You Can't Manage It" Is True… and Misused ▾

The phrase "what gets measured gets managed" is often repeated in risk management as if measurement is binary: either you have precise data or you have nothing. Hubbard's argument is more nuanced and more useful: most measurement starts with uncertainty, and the goal is to reduce uncertainty only to the extent it improves a decision.

Measurement isn't precision. It's decision support.

A risk estimate with wide uncertainty can still be actionable. If the 90% range of losses is $1M–$3M, you manage it one way. If the 90% range is $10M–$200M, you manage it very differently. Even without knowing the exact number, you know which risks deserve attention.

The "value of information" test

One of the most practical ideas in the literature is Value of Information (VOI): what is the expected benefit of reducing uncertainty, and is it worth the cost of measurement? This is how mature risk programs avoid analysis paralysis. Not every risk warrants deep modeling. Some risks warrant a light estimate plus monitoring triggers.

How this applies to ERM materiality

Materiality decisions often fail because they're framed as debates, not decisions. A better approach: define the decision ("is this risk material enough to require appetite metrics and board visibility?"), define the threshold ("material if potential downside exceeds X dollars or Y capital impact with Z probability"), estimate enough to classify, and if uncertain and high-stakes, invest in more measurement. This turns ERM into an operating system instead of a periodic exercise.

The uncomfortable truth: "unquantifiable risk" is often lazy wording

Some risks are genuinely hard to quantify directly—reputation, culture, conduct. But most can be quantified indirectly using proxies: customer attrition cost, funding spread impact, legal settlement distributions, remediation costs, scenario-driven revenue loss. The goal isn't perfect measurement. It's a quantified lens that improves prioritization and tradeoffs.

Practical takeaway

Treat risk measurement as an iterative process: start with a range, identify what drives the range, reduce uncertainty only when the decision warrants it. That's how ERM becomes both rigorous and practical—without becoming an academic exercise.

03 Building a Risk Inventory That Actually Works (and Survives Exams) ▾

Most organizations have some form of "risk inventory." Too often it becomes a list of risk topics with inconsistent definitions, unclear ownership, and no way to decide what's actually material. That's not an inventory—it's a catalog of concerns. Here's what makes a decision-grade risk inventory.

1. A risk statement must be specific enough to evaluate

A strong risk statement typically includes a driver (what changes), a mechanism (how it causes loss), an outcome (what gets worse), and a scope (what business or process). You don't need legal drafting. You need clarity.

2. Ownership must be explicit across lines of defense

A functioning inventory makes accountability visible: first line (business owner accountable for managing exposure) and second line (risk owner accountable for oversight and challenge). If ownership is unclear, remediation stalls. If both lines "own" it vaguely, nobody owns it.

3. Materiality must have a method

Materiality isn't "what feels scary." It's a decision supported by criteria—economic impact thresholds (loss, earnings volatility, capital impact), regulatory sensitivity, operational disruption, speed of onset and controllability. Even if some criteria are qualitative, the method is explicit and repeatable.

4. Don't force false precision

It's better to represent uncertainty honestly than to hide it behind a confident score. Use ranges and scenario-based estimates where appropriate.

5. Governance and challenge are part of the product

An inventory that isn't challenged becomes a political artifact. Mature ERM requires a review cadence, challenge protocols ("what evidence supports this?"), escalation thresholds, and documentation standards. This is what makes the inventory defensible to regulators, auditors, and boards.

Practical takeaway

A strong inventory is standardized, owned, measurable (or proxy-measurable), governed, and repeatable. When those elements are present, ERM becomes a platform the organization can build on: risk appetite, stress testing linkage, board reporting, and strategic decisioning.

04 Incident Lessons Without Blame: Turning Failures Into Controls and Resilience ▾

Operational failures will happen. What matters is whether the organization learns faster than the risk can scale. The goal isn't producing artifacts that feel good—it's producing outputs that change outcomes.

The common failure mode

Many incident postmortems end with vague root causes ("process breakdown"), generic fixes ("improve training"), no measurable control improvements, and no linkage to risk appetite or KRIs. That's how the same incident repeats—often in a bigger form.

A better model: incident → mechanism → controls → monitoring

  • Define the failure mechanism (what exactly broke)
  • Identify control gaps (prevent / detect / respond)
  • Prioritize fixes by expected reduction in loss likelihood or impact
  • Define KRIs that provide early warning
  • Track remediation to closure with evidence

Resilience is a product feature

Resilience isn't a binder. It's the ability to maintain critical services under disruption. That requires embedding dependency mapping, tabletop exercises tied to realistic scenarios, recovery objectives that reflect business priorities, and evidence that the organization can execute under pressure.

Practical takeaway

Build an incident discipline that produces specific mechanisms, targeted controls, measurable monitoring, and closed-loop remediation. That's operational risk management that actually reduces risk—not just reports it.
